The word "trauma" is used widely — but what does it actually mean for the mind and body? And why do some people seem to recover relatively quickly while others carry the weight of a difficult experience for years?

Understanding trauma is not just an academic exercise. It is deeply practical. When we understand what is happening inside us, we can approach healing with more compassion for ourselves — and more clarity about what actually helps.

## What Is Trauma?

Trauma is not defined by the event itself, but by **the impact it has on the person who experiences it**. The same event can be traumatic for one person and not for another, depending on factors like prior history, social support, age at the time, and individual resilience.

Broadly, trauma falls into two categories:

**Single-incident trauma** — A discrete event such as a road accident, assault, natural disaster, or sudden loss.

**Complex trauma** — Repeated or prolonged exposure to threatening or harmful experiences, often in childhood or within relationships. This includes emotional neglect, domestic violence, or growing up in an unpredictable environment.

Both can have lasting effects on how the brain and body function.

## What Happens in the Brain

When we face a threatening situation, the brain's threat-detection centre — the **amygdala** — sounds the alarm. It bypasses the thinking, reasoning parts of the brain (the prefrontal cortex) and activates the survival response almost instantaneously.

This is useful in the moment. But after a traumatic event, the amygdala can remain on high alert long after the threat has passed. It becomes hypersensitive — detecting danger in situations that are actually safe. Ordinary sounds, smells, or situations that resemble the original trauma can trigger the same intense survival response.

This is why trauma survivors often feel as though they are "reliving" the experience rather than simply remembering it. The brain has stored the trauma not as a clear narrative memory, but as fragments of sensation, emotion, and image — without a clear timestamp. The body responds as if the event is happening now.

## Common Responses to Trauma

Trauma can produce a wide range of responses, all of which make sense as survival mechanisms:

**Intrusion** — Flashbacks, nightmares, intrusive images or memories that arrive without warning. The mind is attempting to process what happened, but the processing keeps getting stuck.

**Avoidance** — Staying away from people, places, conversations, or thoughts that serve as reminders. This reduces short-term distress but prevents healing in the long term.

**Hyperarousal** — Feeling constantly on edge, startling easily, having difficulty sleeping, or feeling irritable or angry. The nervous system is stuck in a state of high alert.

**Emotional numbing** — Feeling detached from others, losing interest in things that once mattered, or feeling as though nothing is real. This is the brain's way of protecting itself from being overwhelmed.

**Negative changes in thinking** — Persistent beliefs such as "I am damaged," "I cannot trust anyone," or "The world is completely dangerous." These beliefs often feel like facts, not thoughts.

## The Body Keeps the Score

Trauma does not live only in the mind. It is stored in the body. People who have experienced trauma often describe physical symptoms that have no clear medical explanation — chronic pain, tension, fatigue, gastrointestinal problems, or a persistent feeling of physical unease.

The nervous system, having been pushed into survival mode, can become dysregulated. The body remains in a state of chronic stress, with elevated cortisol affecting sleep, immunity, digestion, and concentration.

This is why effective trauma treatment often addresses both the mind and the body — not just the thoughts and memories, but also the physical experience of being in the body.

## Post-Traumatic Growth

One of the most important things I want my clients to know is this: trauma can change you, but it does not have to define you. Research on **post-traumatic growth** — a concept I explored in depth in my MS dissertation — shows that many people who have experienced significant trauma go on to experience meaningful positive changes:

- A deeper appreciation for life
- Stronger, more authentic relationships
- A greater sense of personal strength
- New possibilities and directions
- Spiritual or existential growth

Post-traumatic growth does not mean the trauma was "worth it," or that the pain was not real. It means that human beings have a remarkable capacity to find meaning and rebuild — especially with the right support.

## What Effective Trauma Treatment Looks Like

Healing from trauma requires a safe therapeutic relationship and an approach that does not push too fast. Key elements include:

**Stabilisation first** — Before processing traumatic memories, building a foundation of safety, grounding skills, and emotional regulation. This ensures you are not overwhelmed during the therapeutic work.

**Trauma-informed CBT** — Working with the thoughts, beliefs, and avoidance patterns that keep trauma responses locked in place. Gradually approaching rather than avoiding the memories, in a controlled and supported way.

**Exposure-based work** — Carefully revisiting traumatic memories in a way that allows the brain to process and file them as past events, rather than present dangers.

**Meaning-making** — Exploring what the experience means for your sense of self, relationships, and future. This is where post-traumatic growth often takes root.

## You Do Not Have to Carry This Alone

If you recognise yourself in any of what is described here, please know that what you are experiencing has a name, a reason, and — most importantly — a path forward. Trauma symptoms are not signs of weakness. They are signs that your mind and body worked hard to protect you during something very difficult.

Reaching out for support is not giving up — it is taking your wellbeing seriously. I work with trauma survivors with deep respect for the courage it takes to begin this process.
