Almost everyone feels anxious at some point. Before an exam, a difficult conversation, or an important event, a sense of nervousness or worry is entirely normal — and even useful. But for millions of people, anxiety goes far beyond these ordinary moments. It becomes persistent, overwhelming, and begins to interfere with daily life.

Understanding the difference between normal anxiety and an anxiety disorder is the first step toward getting the right help.

## What Anxiety Actually Is

Anxiety is your body's natural alarm system. When your brain perceives a threat — real or imagined — it activates the **fight-or-flight response**. Stress hormones like adrenaline and cortisol flood your system. Your heart beats faster, your muscles tense, your breathing quickens, and your senses sharpen. All of this is designed to help you respond to danger.

The problem is that the brain cannot always tell the difference between a genuine threat (a dangerous animal) and a perceived one (a tense conversation at work). It responds the same way to both. When this alarm keeps firing when there is no real danger — or fires so intensely that it becomes debilitating — anxiety has become a problem.

## The Many Faces of Anxiety

Anxiety does not look the same in everyone. It can show up as:

**Generalised Anxiety Disorder (GAD)** — Persistent, excessive worry about many different areas of life (health, finances, relationships, the future). The worry feels uncontrollable and is often accompanied by physical symptoms like muscle tension, fatigue, and difficulty sleeping.

**Social Anxiety** — Intense fear of being judged, embarrassed, or humiliated in social situations. This can lead to significant avoidance of work events, gatherings, or even ordinary conversations.

**Panic Disorder** — Sudden, intense episodes of fear accompanied by heart palpitations, shortness of breath, chest tightness, and a feeling that something terrible is about to happen. Panic attacks are deeply frightening, even when no physical danger exists.

**OCD (Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder)** — Intrusive, unwanted thoughts (obsessions) that drive repetitive behaviours or mental rituals (compulsions) to reduce distress. The relief is temporary, and the cycle continues.

**Health Anxiety** — Persistent worry that one has or will develop a serious illness, despite reassurance from medical professionals.

## Physical Symptoms People Often Miss

Many people experiencing anxiety do not recognise it as anxiety at all — because the symptoms feel entirely physical:

- Chest tightness or palpitations
- Difficulty breathing or a feeling of being smothered
- Dizziness or feeling unsteady
- Nausea or stomach discomfort
- Sweating or trembling
- A sensation of unreality or being detached from yourself

These symptoms are real. They are not imagined. They are the body's genuine physical response to a perceived threat. But when there is no physical cause found after medical investigation, anxiety is often the explanation.

## What Keeps Anxiety Going

The single most powerful thing that maintains anxiety is **avoidance**. When we avoid the thing that makes us anxious, we feel temporary relief — and that relief teaches the brain that avoidance works. So we avoid more. Over time, our world shrinks.

The other major factor is **safety behaviours** — things we do to manage anxiety in the moment that prevent us from learning that we could actually cope. Sitting near the door at a gathering. Always having a phone in hand. Constantly seeking reassurance. These feel helpful, but they quietly confirm to the brain that the situation is dangerous.

## What Helps

Anxiety is one of the most treatable mental health conditions. Effective approaches include:

**Cognitive Behaviour Therapy (CBT)** — Identifying anxious thinking patterns and testing them against reality. Learning to tolerate uncertainty rather than seek constant reassurance.

**Exposure Therapy** — Gradually and systematically facing feared situations, allowing anxiety to peak and then naturally reduce. Each exposure teaches the brain that the feared outcome did not occur — and that you can cope.

**Relaxation and breathing techniques** — Progressive Muscle Relaxation and controlled breathing activate the body's calming system and reduce the physical intensity of anxiety.

**Lifestyle factors** — Sleep, physical activity, reducing caffeine, and social connection all have meaningful effects on anxiety levels.

## When to Seek Help

Consider reaching out to a psychologist if:

- Anxiety is affecting your work, relationships, or daily functioning
- You are avoiding situations that you used to engage in comfortably
- Worry feels uncontrollable and consumes significant time each day
- You are using substances to manage anxious feelings
- Physical symptoms of anxiety are frequent and distressing

Anxiety does not have to run your life. With the right support, most people experience significant and lasting improvement. Reaching out is the hardest step — and also the most important one.
